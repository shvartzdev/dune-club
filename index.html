About

What is this club?

A discussion club that explores the universe of Dune — through film, science, philosophy, and politics. No prior reading required for Season 1. Just curiosity.

We meet session by session, each with a central question and a set of discussion prompts. Season 1 is built around the Villeneuve films. Season 2 dives deeper — into the real science behind the world Frank Herbert imagined.

We use The Science of Dune — a collection of essays by scientists — as our guide to the real physics, biology, and ecology hiding inside Arrakis.

Season I · The World
01

First Impressions & The Vibe

"Why does Dune feel so different from other sci-fi?"

  • 1. What was your first reaction to Dune — movie, book, or both? Did you get it immediately or did it take time?
  • 2. Why does Dune feel so serious compared to Marvel or Star Wars? Is that a feature or a bug?
  • 3. The movie has almost no jokes. Did that bother you or did it feel exactly right?
  • 4. If someone asked "what is Dune about?" in one sentence — what would you say?
  • 5. Hot Take "Dune is just Lawrence of Arabia with sandworms." Agree?
  • 6. Hans Zimmer built entirely new instruments for the score. Does music shape how "alien" a world feels?

02

Paul Atreides — Hero or Warning?

"Is Paul actually the good guy?"

  • 1. Do you root for Paul in the films? When did that feeling shift, if ever?
  • 2. Paul knows the future but keeps making the same choices anyway. Is that brave or stupid?
  • 3. Dune is supposedly a deconstruction of the "chosen one" trope. Did the films actually make Paul feel dangerous rather than cool?
  • 4. Compare Paul to Harry Potter, Neo, Frodo. What's structurally different?
  • 5. Debate Is Paul Atreides a hero, a villain, or a victim?
  • 6. Chalamet plays Paul as fragile and reluctant. Does casting shape how we judge a character morally?
03

Spice, Oil & Real-World Politics

"Dune is basically about oil. Let's talk about that."

  • 1. Herbert wrote Dune in the 1960s partly inspired by Middle Eastern oil politics. What parallels do you see today?
  • 2. The Fremen have lived in the desert forever — then outsiders arrive and say "we need what's under your sand." Does that sound familiar?
  • 3. Is Dune anti-imperialist, or does it still glorify the white outsider who leads a native people?
  • 4. The Atreides are "the good imperialists." Is there such a thing?
  • 5. If spice existed today, which real country or company would control it?
  • 6. Hot Take Every "chosen saviour in a foreign land" blockbuster is secretly about empire. Name one that isn't.

The Women of Dune

"They knew everything and still got treated like props."

  • 1. Lady Jessica, Chani, Reverend Mother Mohiam — who is the most powerful character in the films?
  • 2. The Bene Gesserit have been running a secret eugenics program for thousands of years. Are they the real villains?
  • 3. Chani in the Villeneuve films is very different from the books. Which version do you prefer and why?
  • 4. Is Dune feminist, or does it just have strong women in service of a man's story?
  • 5. Speculation If the Bene Gesserit ran your country, would things actually be better or worse?
  • 6. Zendaya has almost no dialogue in Part 1. Did that absence make Chani more or less interesting?
05

Sandworms — The Mythology of a Monster

"The most iconic creature in sci-fi. Why?"

  • 1. The sandworms are sacred, terrifying, and also the source of all spice. What does that symbolism mean to you?
  • 2. Riding a sandworm is a Fremen rite of passage. What's the equivalent in your culture or life?
  • 3. In the films, worms feel like natural disasters. In the books, they're more like gods. Which version is scarier?
  • 4. Creative Describe a sandworm using only things from your kitchen.
  • 5. If sandworms existed on Earth, where would they live and what would they eat?
  • 6. The worm's sound design won awards. How does sound build a creature's mythology before you even see it?

Villeneuve vs. Lynch vs. the Miniseries

"Three very different Dunes. Let's fight about it."

  • 1. Have you seen the 1984 David Lynch film? What is even happening in that movie?
  • 2. The 2000 Sci-Fi Channel miniseries is extremely cheap and extremely faithful. Does faithfulness matter if the budget is zero?
  • 3. Villeneuve split Part 1 and Part 2 across two films. Was that the right call, or did it break the story's rhythm?
  • 4. What does each adaptation understand about Dune that the others miss?
  • 5. Hot Take The Lynch film is the most "Dune" because it's weird, overwhelming, and makes you feel lost. Agree?
  • 6. Villeneuve shot Part 1 in desaturated blues and Part 2 in warm ambers. What story is the colour palette telling?
07

Religion as a Weapon

"The Bene Gesserit planted prophecies on purpose. That's wild."

  • 1. The Missionaria Protectiva spread religious myths to planets for future manipulation. How do you feel about that?
  • 2. Paul uses Fremen religious belief to build his army. Is he exploiting them or genuinely becoming what they believe?
  • 3. Can you think of historical examples of religion being used as a political tool in similar ways?
  • 4. Is having a fake messiah worse than having a real one?
  • 5. The Fremen believed before Paul arrived. Does it matter if the prophecy was manufactured?
  • 6. Debate Is Paul's holy war an inevitable consequence of religion, or a personal moral failure?

Ecology & Climate — Arrakis as Climate Fiction

"Herbert was writing about climate change before it was a conversation."

  • 1. Arrakis is a desert planet because spice extraction destroys its ecosystem. Sound familiar?
  • 2. The Fremen dream of terraforming Arrakis — turning it green. But the book treats this as a tragedy. Why?
  • 3. Liet-Kynes dies believing in ecological restoration. Is that kind of hope naive or necessary?
  • 4. Creative Pick one real environmental crisis and reframe it in Dune terms.
  • 5. If you had to choose between economic prosperity and ecological preservation (the Arrakis dilemma), what would you choose?
  • 6. The stillsuit is a perfect closed-loop recycling system. What would the equivalent look like for energy or food?
09

Power, Betrayal & Houses

"Nobody in Dune has a single loyal friend."

  • 1. The Imperium is built on betrayal being more reliable than loyalty. Does that reflect how real politics works?
  • 2. Who is the biggest traitor in Dune — and were they justified?
  • 3. The Great Houses are noble families running everything. Compare to modern corporations, royal families, or dynasties.
  • 4. Game Assign each House (Atreides, Harkonnen, Corrino) to a real-world country, company, or party. Defend your choices.
  • 5. If you had to swear loyalty to one House, which and why?
  • 6. CHOAM runs all trade in the Imperium. Is "a galaxy run by one monopoly" more or less believable than our own economy?

Baron Harkonnen — Villain, Satirical Icon, or Both?

"He's horrifying and also kind of hilarious. That's deliberate."

  • 1. The Baron floats. He eats people. He monologues. Is he scary or campy?
  • 2. Stellan Skarsgård vs. Ian McNeice vs. Kenneth McMillan — best performance?
  • 3. The Harkonnens are coded as decadent, excessive, and cruel. What is Herbert actually criticising through them?
  • 4. Is Feyd-Rautha the dark mirror of Paul, or his own thing entirely?
  • 5. Hot Take The Harkonnens are actually more honest about power than the Atreides. Discuss.
  • 6. Austin Butler plays Feyd entirely in black-and-white scenes. How did that aesthetic shape your perception of him?
Season II · The Science
11

Real Sandworms? The Biology of Extreme Life

"Life exists where we thought it couldn't."

  • 1. Extremophiles survive in boiling vents, radioactive water, pure acid. How close is Earth already to Arrakis biology?
  • 2. A 400-metre sandworm would collapse under its own weight. What real biology would need to change for one to exist?
  • 3. The worms produce spice as a metabolic byproduct. Real organisms produce compounds we use as drugs. Any parallels?
  • 4. The Fremen believe the worms are divine. Does science and religion conflict in how we treat endangered species?
  • 5. Speculation Design a real-world organism that fills the same ecological niche as a sandworm.

Could Arrakis Exist? Desert Planets & Astronomy

"The universe is mostly hostile. Arrakis might be generous."

  • 1. Arrakis orbits two suns. We've found real planets in multi-star systems. What would living under two suns actually feel like?
  • 2. A planet with no large water bodies but a breathable atmosphere — is that physically possible? What drives the water cycle?
  • 3. The chapter "The Real Stars of Dune" maps the Dune universe onto real star positions. Does grounding sci-fi in real science make it better or worse?
  • 4. We've found desert exoplanets in habitable zones. If we discovered one, should we try to settle it?
  • 5. Debate The real challenge of Arrakis isn't the heat — it's the psychological effect of scarcity. Agree?
13

Engineering Survival — The Stillsuit Problem

"Your body wastes 2 litres of water a day. Can we fix that?"

  • 1. The stillsuit recycles all body moisture — sweat, urine, breath. Is that actually feasible with real materials science?
  • 2. The Fremen's whole culture is shaped by water scarcity. What does that tell us about how environment shapes society?
  • 3. We already have atmospheric water generators that pull water from desert air. Is the stillsuit closer to reality than we think?
  • 4. Spitting in someone's presence is an insult on Arrakis. What physical substances do we consider sacred or taboo — and why?
  • 5. Design If you were building a survival suit for a real desert planet, what three features would you prioritise?

Prescience, Memory & The Mind

"The spice unlocks something in the brain. What exactly?"

  • 1. Prescience in Dune is less about seeing the future and more about seeing probability. Is that a scientific or mystical concept?
  • 2. Spice expands consciousness. Real psychedelics also alter perception of time. Is Herbert describing a real neurological mechanism?
  • 3. The Mentats are human computers, trained to replace AI after the Butlerian Jihad. How much of human cognition could actually be optimised through training?
  • 4. The Tleilaxu use genetic memory — memories passed through DNA. Is there any real science behind inherited trauma or memory?
  • 5. Debate If you could take a substance that made you more intelligent but caused terrible visions of violence — would you?

The Science of Dune

What's Real on Arrakis?

Frank Herbert spent years researching ecology, religion, politics, and physics. The book's scientists took him seriously. Here's what holds up.

🪱

Sandworm Biology

Real extremophiles survive in conditions nearly as hostile as Arrakis. The worm's tube-segment anatomy mirrors real annelid biology scaled to the impossible.

🌊

Dune Formation

Ralph Lorenz studies real dunes on Titan and Mars. Arrakis's dune patterns follow real aeolian physics — barchans, seifs, draa. Herbert got the geography right.

🧬

Genetics & Breeding

The Bene Gesserit breeding program mirrors real selective pressure. "From Silver Fox to Kwisatz Haderach" traces the science from 1950s fox domestication to genetic destiny.

✨

Spice & Consciousness

Melange is a real-world composite: psychedelics, antiaging compounds, and nootropics. Real neuroscience partially supports what Herbert imagined spice does to the brain.

🌌

Space Folding

"Cosmic Origami" explores whether folding space — collapsing the distance between two points — has any basis in real physics. (Spoiler: barely. But barely is enough.)

🔮

Prescience & Prophecy

Pattern recognition, probability calculation, and altered states of consciousness. Paul's visions look increasingly like what neuroscientists call "future simulation."

💧

Stillsuit Engineering

Closed-loop water recycling from body fluids. Membrane science has come far enough that this is now an engineering problem, not a physics impossibility.

⭐

Arrakis Astronomy

Kevin Grazier maps the Dune universe onto real stellar coordinates. Binary star systems that could host desert planets in habitable zones actually exist.
